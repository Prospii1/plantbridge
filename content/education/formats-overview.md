# Cannabis Formats: A Practical Overview

How you consume cannabis significantly affects the onset time, duration, intensity, and character of the experience. Understanding the major formats helps you make more intentional choices aligned with your wellness goals.

## Inhaled: Flower

**What it is:** Dried cannabis plant material consumed by combustion (smoking) or vaporization.

**Onset:** 2–10 minutes  
**Duration:** 1–3 hours  
**Key consideration:** Combustion produces byproducts (carbon monoxide, particulates) that some users prefer to avoid. Vaporization heats flower below combustion temperatures and is generally considered a cleaner delivery method.

**Best for:** Users who want fast onset and easier real-time titration. Because effects come on quickly, it is easier to gauge your response before consuming more.

## Inhaled: Vaporized Concentrates

**What it is:** Concentrated cannabis extracts (oil, wax, shatter, live resin) consumed via vaporization devices.

**Onset:** 2–5 minutes  
**Duration:** 1–3 hours  
**Key consideration:** Concentrates are significantly more potent than flower. Terpene retention varies by extraction method — live resin and rosin tend to preserve more of the original plant's terpene profile.

**Best for:** Experienced users seeking a more potent, terpene-rich experience.

## Sublingual: Tinctures and Oils

**What it is:** Liquid extracts held under the tongue (sublingually) for 60–90 seconds before swallowing. Cannabinoids absorb through the mucous membranes directly into the bloodstream.

**Onset:** 15–45 minutes  
**Duration:** 2–4 hours  
**Key consideration:** More consistent and predictable than edibles because absorption bypasses first-pass liver metabolism (at least partially). Dose control is precise using a graduated dropper.

**Best for:** Users who want faster onset than edibles with more controlled dosing. A good middle ground between inhalation and oral consumption.

## Oral: Edibles

**What it is:** Cannabis-infused food and beverages. THC is converted to a more potent metabolite (11-hydroxy-THC) during digestion.

**Onset:** 45–120 minutes  
**Duration:** 4–8 hours (sometimes longer)  
**Key consideration:** The most common format for overconsumption. Because onset is slow, many users consume more before feeling effects, leading to an unexpectedly intense experience. Always wait at least 2 hours before considering an additional dose.

**Best for:** Extended, longer-lasting wellness support. Also preferred by users who want to avoid inhalation entirely.

## Oral: Capsules and Softgels

**What it is:** Pre-measured cannabis oil in pill form, processed like edibles.

**Onset:** 45–90 minutes  
**Duration:** 4–6 hours  
**Key consideration:** Highly consistent dosing — no guesswork compared to homemade edibles. Onset slightly faster than some edibles due to formulation differences.

**Best for:** Users who prefer a pharmaceutical-style delivery with precise, repeatable doses.

## Topical: Creams, Balms, and Salves

**What it is:** Cannabis-infused products applied directly to the skin.

**Onset:** 15–45 minutes (varies by formulation)  
**Duration:** 2–4 hours locally  
**Key consideration:** Standard topicals are generally non-intoxicating — cannabinoids do not reach the bloodstream in significant quantities through skin absorption. Transdermal patches are a different category and can produce systemic effects.

**Best for:** Localized comfort support. No psychoactive effects with standard topicals.

## Choosing a Format

Consider these factors when evaluating formats:
- **How quickly do you need onset?** → Inhaled or sublingual
- **How long do you want effects to last?** → Oral formats offer longer duration
- **Do you want to avoid intoxication entirely?** → Topicals or high-CBD formulations
- **Is consistent dosing important?** → Capsules or tinctures over edibles
