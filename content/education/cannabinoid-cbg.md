# CBG (Cannabigerol)

CBG is sometimes called the "mother cannabinoid" because the plant produces CBGA (the acid form) first — and then converts it into the precursors for THC, CBD, and CBC. Most cannabis strains contain only small amounts of CBG in the final product, making CBG-rich extracts more expensive to produce.

## How CBG Interacts with the Body

Unlike CBD, CBG binds more directly to both CB1 and CB2 receptors in the endocannabinoid system. CB2 receptors are found in high density in immune tissue, which is why CBG has attracted interest in inflammation and gut-related research. CBG also appears to interact with alpha-2 adrenergic receptors and may inhibit the reuptake of anandamide — one of the body's naturally occurring endocannabinoids.

## What Research Suggests

CBG research is early-stage but promising:

- **Inflammation** — Preclinical studies suggest CBG may support the body's natural response to inflammation, particularly in gut tissue
- **Mood and anxiety** — Early research points to potential calming effects, possibly through its interaction with serotonin receptors
- **Neuroprotection** — Some animal studies have looked at CBG's potential to support neuronal health, though human studies are limited
- **Focus and alertness** — Some users report a clear-headed, focused quality from CBG, contrasting with the sedating effects associated with some other cannabinoids

## Formats

CBG is most commonly found as:
- **Tinctures and oils** — the most widely available form
- **Capsules** — for consistent daily dosing
- **Flower** — hemp cultivars bred for high CBG content

Look for products that list CBG prominently on the certificate of analysis (COA) rather than just on the label.

## Starting Guidance

CBG is generally considered well-tolerated. A common educational starting point is 5–10 mg daily, increasing gradually over 1–2 weeks. Effects are typically subtle at low doses.
