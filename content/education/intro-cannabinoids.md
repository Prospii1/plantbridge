# Understanding Cannabinoids

Cannabinoids are naturally occurring chemical compounds found in the cannabis plant. Over 100 distinct cannabinoids have been identified, each with unique properties and potential interactions with the human body.

## The Endocannabinoid System

The human body contains a biological network called the endocannabinoid system (ECS). This system plays a role in regulating a range of processes including mood, sleep, appetite, memory, and how we experience discomfort. The ECS consists of:

- **Endocannabinoids** — molecules your body produces naturally (such as anandamide and 2-AG)
- **Receptors** — primarily CB1 receptors (concentrated in the brain and nervous system) and CB2 receptors (found in immune tissues throughout the body)
- **Enzymes** — which break down endocannabinoids after they've served their purpose

Plant-derived cannabinoids from cannabis — called phytocannabinoids — may interact with this system in ways that researchers are actively studying.

## The Major Cannabinoids

**THC (Tetrahydrocannabinol)** is the primary psychoactive compound in cannabis. It binds strongly to CB1 receptors, which is responsible for its mind-altering effects. Research suggests it may support relaxation, comfort, and appetite in some individuals.

**CBD (Cannabidiol)** is non-intoxicating and has attracted significant scientific interest. Unlike THC, CBD does not bind strongly to CB1 receptors. Research suggests it may support a sense of calm and general wellness. One FDA-approved CBD medication (Epidiolex) exists for specific seizure conditions — though this is not a claim about recreational or wellness CBD products.

**CBN (Cannabinol)** forms as THC ages and breaks down. Users often explore CBN-forward products for evening wind-down routines, though research is still emerging.

**CBG (Cannabigerol)** is sometimes called the "mother cannabinoid" because other cannabinoids are synthesized from its acidic form. Early research is exploring its potential properties.

## The Entourage Effect

Research suggests that cannabinoids may work more effectively in combination with each other and with terpenes — a concept sometimes called the "entourage effect." This is why full-spectrum and broad-spectrum products may produce different experiences than isolated cannabinoids.

## A Note on Individual Response

Cannabinoids affect people differently based on genetics, tolerance, metabolism, method of consumption, and many other factors. What works well for one person may not work the same way for another. Starting low and going slow is a principle many experienced users follow.
