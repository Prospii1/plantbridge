# Beta-Caryophyllene

Beta-caryophyllene (BCP) is one of the most abundant terpenes in cannabis and is also found in black pepper, cloves, rosemary, and hops. It has an unusual property that sets it apart from almost all other terpenes: it binds directly to CB2 receptors in the endocannabinoid system. This makes it technically a dietary cannabinoid as well as a terpene.

## How It Works

Because BCP activates CB2 receptors — which are concentrated in the immune system and peripheral tissues rather than the brain — it does not produce psychoactive effects. CB2 activation is associated with the body's natural inflammatory response regulation and immune signaling.

## What Research Suggests

BCP is one of the more studied terpenes:

- **Inflammation support** — Multiple preclinical studies show BCP may help modulate the body's inflammatory response
- **Discomfort** — Animal studies suggest analgesic properties, possibly through CB2 and opioid receptor pathways; human research is ongoing
- **Mood** — Some research suggests CB2 receptor activation may influence mood and stress responses; BCP has been studied in the context of anxiolytic effects in animal models
- **Gut health** — BCP appears to have a protective role in gut tissue in several preclinical studies

## The "Entourage" Angle

BCP is considered a significant contributor to the entourage effect in full-spectrum cannabis products. Its CB2-binding activity complements CBD (which works through different pathways), and the combination may produce synergistic effects greater than either compound alone.

## Where to Find It

Look for BCP on the terpene section of a product's COA. Common strains/cultivars high in BCP include those with spicy, peppery aroma notes. It is also available as a dietary supplement (often from clove or black pepper extract).

## Is It Psychoactive?

No. Unlike CB1-binding cannabinoids, CB2 activation does not produce intoxication. BCP is considered non-psychoactive at the concentrations found in cannabis products.
